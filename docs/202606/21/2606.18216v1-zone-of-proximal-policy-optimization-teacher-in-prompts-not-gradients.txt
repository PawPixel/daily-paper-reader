Abstract 

Knowledge distillation transfers a teacher’s competence to a small student but is brittle in the small-student regime: forcing the student to imitate logits from a much larger teacher concentrates it on the teacher’s sharpest modes, hurting generalization on benchmark families beyond the training corpus. Reinforcement learning (RL) avoids logit imitation by training on the student’s own rollouts. However, on questions where every rollout fails—yielding zero advantage and being silently discarded—injecting a stronger teacher’s response into the policy gradient breaks the on-policy assumption and induces drift. We introduce Zone of Proximal Policy Optimization (ZPPO), inspired by Vygotsky’s zone of proximal development , which keeps the teacher inside the prompt rather than the policy gradient. On hard questions, ZPPO constructs two reformulated prompts: a Binary Candidate-included Question (BCQ) pairs one correct teacher response with one incorrect student response as anonymized candidates the student must discriminate, and a Negative Candidate-included Question (NCQ) aggregates the student’s wrong rollouts into a single prompt to surface their shared failure modes. A prompt replay buffer recirculates each hard question until it either graduates – the student’s mean rollout accuracy on it reaches half – or is FIFO-evicted under finite capacity, amplifying BCQ and NCQ inside the student’s current zone of proximal development. On the Qwen3.5 family at four student scales (0.8B–9B) with a 27B teacher, post-trained as vision–language models and evaluated on a 31-benchmark suite (16 VLM, 10 LLM, 5 Video), ZPPO outperforms off/on-policy distillation and GRPO, with the largest gains at the smallest scale. 1. Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) and vision–language models (VLMs) have made rapid progress [ 1– 5 ]on broad benchmarks, with reinforcement learning post-training [ 5– 10 ]. The cost of those gains, however, has scaled with the cost of the model: tens of billions of activated parameters. Nearly all of the gains we see at the frontier do not transport to the regime that matters for deployment on mobile phones, AR/VR glasses, or embodied robots [ 11 – 16 ], where compute and memory budgets are much smaller. Closing this deployment gap requires small models that generalize broadly, not narrow specialists. The standard knowledge-transfer recipe is knowledge distillation : a small student is trained to imitate the logits, hidden states, or sampled outputs of a strong teacher [ 17 – 21 ]. Despite its effectiveness, distillation carries a structural limitation that becomes severe when the student is much smaller than the teacher. Logit imitation is, at its core, supervised fitting on the teacher’s distribution: with limited training data, students memorize teacher answers [ 22 –24 ], and the limited capacity ( e.g. ,“mode-seeking bias”) concentrates the student on the teacher’s sharpest peaks [ 20 , 25 ]. The result is a student whose generalization on benchmark families beyond the training corpus is brittle, and whose responses rarely venture beyond the paths the teacher has already taken (Tab. 2). A line of work tries to alleviate these issues by using the student’s own rollouts to conduct distillation from the teacher to the student ( on-policy distillation [ 25 –31 ]) or by removing the external teacher and letting the student teach itself ( self -distillation [ 32 –34 , 22 , 35 ]). These reduce the teacher–student capacity gap, but they all share the same fundamental constraint: the training signal is still a logit distribution that the student must match. For 0.8B or 2B students, model capacity is too limited to absorb such a distribution broadly, and the resulting models lose generalization on benchmarks beyond the narrow training data [22–24]. A natural alternative is reinforcement learning (RL) [ 5 , 36 , 37 , 8 , 7, 9, 10 , 38 – 41 ], which avoids logit imitation entirely and trains directly on the student’s own rollouts. Fig. 2a summarizes these two failure modes. In this paper, we ask a single question: how can we transfer the teacher’s knowledge to the student without ever imitating the teacher’s logits or injecting the teacher’s response directly into the student’s policy gradient? Our answer, which we call Zone of Proximal Policy Optimization (ZPPO), borrows its name from Vygotsky’s zone of proximal development [42 ] – the band of tasks a learner cannot yet solve alone but can solve with a small amount of guidance from a more capable peer. Concretely, ZPPO keeps the teacher only inside the prompt , never inside the student’s policy gradient (Fig. 2b,c and Fig. 3): 

• For each hard question (defined as one where the student’s mean rollout accuracy is below half), we construct a reformulated prompt – a Binary Candidate-included Question (BCQ) – that appends one correct teacher response and one wrong student response as shuffled, anonymized candidates, and asks the student to solve the problem afresh while consulting them. Since the student generates its own response, its policy gradient remains on-policy at the response level. 

• In parallel, we construct a Negative Candidate-included Question (NCQ) that appends all of the student’s wrong responses, explicitly shows the student that all of them are wrong, and asks for a correct answer. Within independent rollout groups, rollouts cannot “see” each other’s wrong answers; inside our training loop, NCQ is the first place at which a student’s failed attempts become collectively visible to the student, turning independent negatives into a shared signal.

2. Related Work 

Three lines of work bear on ZPPO. Knowledge distillation —whether off-policy, on-policy, or self-distillation [ 18 , 21 , 25 , 26 , 43 , 32 ]—shares a single training signal, a logit distribution the student must match, which recent analyses show becomes brittle when the student is much smaller than the teacher, inducing memorization and mode-seeking [ 22 –24 ]. RL post-training with rule-based rewards on PPO and group-relative variants [ 44 , 36 , 8, 5] has advanced reasoning, but leaves the small-student failure mode where every rollout fails and the group advantage is zero [ 8 ] unaddressed; hybrid RL +distillation fixes [ 45 , 46 ] target this mode, but route teacher responses into the student’s policy gradient through an auxiliary distillation term, which can re-introduce drift. Prompt replay, curriculum, and hint scaffolding [ 47 – 52 ] re-prioritize or scaffold hard questions, but each path compromises a different channel: prompt replay and curriculum [ 47 – 49 ] re-expose the same hard prompt without adding any new signal about why the previous attempt failed; hint methods [ 52 ]append a directional cue (without the answer) that the student tends to copy as a shortcut without necessarily internalizing why the direction is correct; and prefix methods [ 50 , 51 ] insert part of the teacher’s response as a prefix into the student’s rollout: the student is trained on off-policy trajectories conditioned on teacher response. ZPPO differs from all three by moving teacher knowledge from the gradient to the prompt via two reformulations (BCQ and NCQ), so every response is sampled from the student’s own policy, with the prompt replay buffer amplifying inside the student’s zone of proximal development [42]. In a standard rollout group, each wrong rollout contributes its own advantage to the student’s policy gradient independently, and – within independent rollout groups – the student has no way to see patterns across its failures. Within our training loop, NCQ is the first place at which independently sampled wrong rollouts on the same question converge into a single prompt: confronted with its own failed attempts, the student is cued to recognize the shared error patterns and avoid them. 

3.3. Integration with Prompt Replay Buffer 

The prompt replay buffer ℬ exists solely to amplify BCQ and NCQ on questions the student has not yet mastered. It stores only the question 𝑥 (image and text), never any rollout responses. 

(i) Admission and graduation: After each training step we update ℬ from the current rollout batch: a question 𝑥 is admitted if its ¯𝑟 𝑥 < 0.5, and an already-admitted 𝑥 is graduated (removed) on any later step where ¯𝑟 𝑥 reaches half ( ¯𝑟 𝑥 ≥ 0.5). Because BCQ and NCQ are constructed only on hard questions, every replayed question is eligible for one or both reformulations on its next visit. The buffer therefore always tracks the student’s current zone of proximal development. 

(ii) Sampling and capacity: Each rollout batch combines new questions from the data loader with replay samples drawn uniformly from ℬ, where the replay count is a fixed fraction 𝜌 replay of the new-question count. From the union of new and replayed questions, BCQ and NCQ are constructed on the hardest ones first (ranked by ascending ¯𝑟 𝑥 ); the combined BCQ+NCQ count per rollout step is then capped at a fraction 𝜌 aug of the new-question count (Appendix C). 4.4. Component ablation 

Tab. 3 isolates each ZPPO component on the 16 VLM benchmarks ( 4B and 9B in Tab. 12). Starting from GRPO, we incrementally add the buffer ( †), both reformulations together ( Both = BCQ + NCQ), and each reformulation paired with the buffer. 

(i) Each isolated component is modest. Replay alone (GRPO †) and reformulation alone (GRPO + Both) move only a small margin over GRPO at every scale – useful, but not sufficient. 

(ii) Replay × reformulation is super-additive. Pairing BCQ or NCQ with the buffer compounds far beyond the sum of isolated effects, because the buffer keeps re-exposing each hard question to a fresh candidate set per visit. 

(iii) The full recipe is strongest at every scale. ZPPO ties or achieves the column max on every benchmark of Tab. 3 and Tab. 12. Within the recipe, BCQ’s contribution shrinks with scale and NCQ’s grows – mirroring Fig. 5a – so weaker students lean on the teacher candidate while stronger students lean on collective failure analysis. 4.5. Additional RL recipe choices 

Beyond BCQ, NCQ, and the buffer, two seemingly minor recipe choices materially shape small-student RL. We isolate them at 2B across all three benchmark groups in Fig. 6. (i) Iterations per step trade off-policy drift against rollout cost; 𝐼 = 4 is the sweet spot. Larger 𝐼 

grows in-step drift: each minibatch pushes the gradient further from the current policy. GRPO’s default 𝐼 = 16 [36 , 8] runs 4× as many updates as 𝐼 = 4 but yields only a marginal gain over the original 2B base (Fig. 6a–c); 𝐼 = 1 removes drift but undertrains at matched step count. 𝐼 = 4 pays a small drift cost but performs four updates per step – the trade that peaks on all three (Fig. 6a–c). 

(ii) Excluding zero-advantage groups from batch normalization (Norm w/o Zero) is critical. Norm w/o Zero (ZPPO default) consistently beats No norm , while Norm w/ Zero clearly degrades below both (Fig. 6d–f). Both ‘Norm’ variants share REINFORCE++ two-step form [ 37 ]; they differ only in whether zero-advantage groups are used (Appendix C, Eqs. 2–3). Trivial groups (all-correct/all-wrong) carry no learning signal, but their zero advantage values shrink the batch standard deviation that normalizes every other advantage, causing inflated advantages.

4.6. Discussion and Analysis (i) Teacher size matters: A larger teacher widens ZPPO’s gain (Fig. 7); shrinking the teacher to 

4B/ 9B collapses the gain toward the GRPO †+NCQ level, since BCQ requires the teacher to succeed where the student fails, and that pool of teacher-correct rollouts dries up as the teacher gets smaller. The drop is steepest at 0.8B, where ZPPO’s gain is most BCQ-dominated (Sec. 4.4). 

(ii) Hint, Prefix vs. BCQ: We compare BCQ against two prompt-side teacher-guidance families: 

Hint appends an answer-free hint to the prompt; and Prefix forces a teacher response prefix into the student and lets the student generate the rest (Appendix F.5). Tab. 4 shows that Hint (Algorithm 5) helps VLM but transfers less to the LLM/Video evaluation suite, since teacher hints act as a directional shortcut without why the direction is correct; Prefix (Algorithm 6) transfers even less because the teacher prefix is off-policy, so drift accumulates over training. BCQ alone beats both because the student is trained to select between two anonymized candidates while reasoning, whereas Hint conditions the prompt on a labeled teacher-derived hint and Prefix routes a teacher-generated prefix into the response itself; full ZPPO opens the widest gap across all.

5. Conclusion 

Distillation directly imitates teacher logits and teacher prefix-injection RL splices teacher tokens into the policy gradient, breaking the on-policy assumption and weakening generalization at small student scales. ZPPO answers our research question by keeping the teacher only inside the prompt, so the student’s gradient stays on its own rollouts while still benefiting from teacher knowledge. The flip side is a structural ceiling: questions on which both teacher and student fail admit no BCQ candidate and leave only NCQ, whose contribution alone is limited – the zone collapses to the NCQ-only regime. Extending the student’s zone beyond what the current teacher covers is, in our view, an important open problem this work leaves behind.

Limitations 

Teacher-bounded zone. The most fundamental limitation of ZPPO is the one we identify in Sec. 5: BCQ requires the teacher to succeed on a hard question, so questions on which both teacher and student fail admit no BCQ candidate and leave only NCQ, whose contribution alone is more limited (Tab. 3, Sec. 4.4). At the upper end of the student-capability range, BCQ’s contribution shrinks accordingly and the available zone effectively collapses to the NCQ-only regime. Extending the zone beyond what the current teacher covers – via curated synthetic prompts, ensembles of teachers, or curriculum-aware question selection – is, in our view, the most important open problem this work leaves behind. 

Tension with dynamic sampling. Dynamic sampling [ 8 ] simply deletes any question where the student got every rollout correct or every rollout wrong. On the other hand, ZPPO does the opposite: those all-wrong questions are exactly the ones we store in the replay buffer, so that BCQ and NCQ can rewrite the prompt and give the student a fresh chance. Hence, naively combining ZPPO and dynamic sampling is not trivial. A natural fix is to run them in sequence: first identify hard questions and only call dynamic sampling if their BCQ- and NCQ-reformulated rollouts still come back all-correct or all-wrong – that is, apply dynamic sampling on top of BCQ and NCQ. We leave this hybrid algorithm to future work. 

Scope beyond reasoning correctness. ZPPO post-trains an already-built compact VLM and is orthogonal to how such small models are first constructed – via architectural designs that compress visual–linguistic computation into small backbones [ 53 –57 ] or via large-to-small distillation of strong teachers [ 58 – 60 ] – so it could in principle be stacked on any of these as a later post-training stage. Several other axes are deliberately out of scope. Extending the recipe to multi-step and agentic multimodal reasoning [ 61 –63 ] is open; we neither evaluate nor train for robustness, which carries its own substantial literature on robust and causal training [ 64 –68 ]; we do not consider inference-time efficiency techniques such as visual-token pruning [ 69 ] that are orthogonal to the training recipe; we do not target conversational or social competence [ 70 ]; we do not touch the upstream perception stacks [ 71 ] that compact models ultimately depend on for embodied deployment; and our evaluation targets single-turn reasoning accuracy rather than complementary axes such as multi-sensor perception [ 72 ], multi-turn dialogue [ 73 ], or iterative self-refinement [ 74 ]. Combining ZPPO with these complementary directions is left to future work. 

Ethical Considerations 

ZPPO is a post-training recipe that builds on the publicly released Qwen3.5 family (open-weight VLMs at 0.8B– 27 B scale) [ 4]. We do not modify or filter the base models’ pretraining data; any social, demographic, linguistic, or modality biases present in the upstream pretraining of Qwen3.5 are inherited by the post-trained students. Our reward signal (rule-based exact-match grader plus LLM-as-a-judge for free-form questions) targets answer correctness on reasoning benchmarks, not safety, fairness, or harm reduction, so ZPPO is not a substitute for dedicated safety alignment such as RLHF, Constitutional AI, or red-teaming. Practitioners deploying ZPPO-trained checkpoints should pair them with an upstream safety alignment stage and downstream content moderation appropriate to the deployment domain. Beyond inherited pretraining bias, dataset-level bias and spurious visual–linguistic correlations are a distinct concern for multimodal models, addressed by debiasing and causal approaches [ 75 , 76 ]; ZPPO’s correctness-only reward neither introduces nor corrects such biases, so these directions remain complementary to our recipe.

A. Full Related Work 

This appendix expands the condensed discussion in Sec. 2 into the three full paragraphs that motivate ZPPO. 

Knowledge distillation for LLMs and VLMs. The dominant recipe for transferring a stronger model’s competence to a smaller one is knowledge distillation, which trains the student to imitate the teacher’s logits, hidden states, or sampled outputs [ 18 ]. In off-policy distillation, the target sequence is fixed – a ground-truth response [ 17 , 19 ] or a teacher rollout – and the student is trained to match the teacher’s per-token logits, with recent variants adaptively scheduling student-generated outputs to balance the training-inference mismatch [ 21 ]. In on-policy distillation, the student instead samples its own response during training, that sequence is fed back into the teacher to obtain target logits, and the student is trained to match them [ 20 , 25 – 31 ]. Self-distillation removes the external teacher, instead using the student itself as the imitation target – either via best-of-𝑁 

rejection sampling on its own rollouts [ 43 , 77 ], or by conditioning the same model on privileged information (e.g., demonstrations, verified traces, or environment feedback) to act as an in-context self-teacher [ 32 – 35 , 22 ]. All three families lean on the same training signal, a logit distribution the student must match, and a recent line of analyses shows that this signal turns brittle once the student is much smaller than the teacher: the student memorizes, mode-seeks, or loses general capability beyond the training data [ 22 –24 ]. ZPPO leaves this regime altogether. The teacher never enters the student’s policy gradient and shows up only as a candidate inside the prompt. 

Reinforcement learning post-training for reasoning. RL post-training built on PPO [ 44 ]and group-relative variants such as GRPO [ 36 ] and DAPO [ 8] has, with rule-based rewards, pushed reasoning on math, science, and broad knowledge tasks [ 5– 7, 9 , 10 , 38 , 39 , 37 , 40 , 41 ], and the same recipe extends to multimodal reasoning on image–question pairs [ 78 – 84 ]. A complementary line scales RL compute either by prolonging training steps or by broadening rollouts per prompt to extend exploration [ 38 , 39 ], regulates entropy to prevent the policy from collapsing onto its current peaks [ 85 , 86 ], and probes whether RL extends a model’s reasoning frontier or merely sharpens what the model already knows [ 87 ]. However, these advances do not solve the small student failure mode on questions where the student’s every rollout still fails, the group advantage stays zero, and the question is silently filtered out as a zero-advantage sample [ 8]. The natural fix – replacing a failed student rollout with a teacher’s correct response inside the policy gradient – breaks the on-policy assumption and induces severe policy drift; recent hybrid RL +distillation lines [ 45 , 46 ] keep the teacher in the policy gradient and therefore inherit the same brittleness. ZPPO instead moves teacher knowledge from the gradient to the prompt : BCQ and NCQ inject teacher and self-generated candidates as reformulated prompts on those same hard questions, so every response token that enters the policy gradient is student-generated (on-policy at the response level). 

Prompt replay, curriculum, and hint scaffolding for RL. A growing body of work re-introduces previously seen questions or rollouts in the spirit of classical experience replay [ 47 ,48 , 88 –90 ], or arranges questions by difficulty from easy to hard [ 49 , 91 ]. Both lines re-prioritize hard questions but neither provides the guidance that distinguishes Vygotsky’s zone of proximal development [ 42 ] from mere difficulty ordering. A complementary line scaffolds the student through difficult problems by injecting hints: prefix-based methods seed a partial reasoning trace into the response so the rollout is part teacher and the policy gradient is no longer strictly on-policy [ 50 , 51 , 92 ], while prompt-injected hints add pre-authored guidance to the prompt only, keeping the response on-policy and optionally correcting the resulting off-policy bias by importance sampling [ 52 , 93 ]. 

> 25 Zone of Proximal Policy Optimization

ZPPO is also a prompt-side method on the same hard questions, but its in-prompt content is BCQ and NCQ candidates assembled at training time from the current rollout batch – not pre-authored hint text – so candidates change dynamically as fresh rollouts are sampled throughout training, with the prompt replay buffer existing solely to amplify these reformulated prompts on questions inside the student’s current zone of proximal development.

C. ZPPO algorithm 

Algorithm 1 summarizes one ZPPO training step. The key invariant is that every response used in the policy gradient is generated by the current student. The frozen teacher is queried only to provide prompt-side candidate text for BCQ (and to compress candidates under a shared compression prompt and token cap), so teacher-generated tokens never enter the gradient as response tokens. ZPPO inherits its RL backbone from three lines of recent work and adds four novel components. 

Inherited from GRPO [ 36 ]: (G1) group-relative advantage formulation; (G2) PPO-style clipped surrogate objective. Inherited from DAPO [ 8]: (Da1) asymmetric clip-higher with (𝜖 low , 𝜖 high ) = (0 .20 , 0.28) ; (Da2) token-level loss aggregation; (Da3) no KL penalty against a reference policy. 

Inherited from REINFORCE++ [ 37 ]: (R1) two-step advantage estimator (Eqs. 2–3). Our recipe choice on top of REINFORCE++ : (Z0) zero-advantage-group exclusion from the batch-statistics computation in Step 2, ablated in Sec. 4.5(ii) and derived in closed form in Eqs. 4–6. ZPPO’s own contributions : (Z1) BCQ – the anonymized binary-candidate prompt reformulation that turns a hard question into a discriminative one-of-two judgment between a correct teacher trace and a wrong student trace, both compressed by the same teacher under a shared compression prompt and token cap to mitigate obvious surface cues in the discrimination; (Z2) NCQ – the collective-negative-candidate prompt reformulation that surfaces all of the student’s wrong rollouts on the same question, together with their parsed final answers, so the student must avoid the wrong rollouts it has just produced; (Z3) prompt replay buffer with hard-question admission, graduation by mean rollout accuracy, and FIFO eviction; (Z4) the super-additive combination of (Z3) with (Z1)+(Z2) that is the central empirical claim of the paper (Sec. 4.4). Algorithm 1 annotates each step with the tags above. A few practical notes are worth pointing out. First, BCQ requires both a correct teacher rollout and a wrong student rollout to construct a candidate pair; questions on which the teacher itself fails on every 𝑦 T skip BCQ for that visit and only contribute NCQ (or, if all student rollouts succeed on the second try, neither). Second, candidate compression runs in parallel with Stage 1 scoring, so it does not stall the rollout loop. Third, the teacher rollouts {𝑦 T} on a replayed question are re-sampled on every visit – the BCQ candidate seen by the student on visit 𝑘 is not the same as the one seen on visit 𝑘 −1, even when the underlying question is identical. This freshness is what allows the buffer to avoid degenerating into a single fixed exemplar per hard question. Fourth, only the plain student rollouts on the original 𝑥 feed buffer admission and graduation in Stage 6: the BCQ/NCQ rollouts contribute to the gradient but are skipped when computing the per-question accuracy used to decide buffer membership, so a question is judged “mastered” only when the student can solve it without 

candidate references. Fifth, the three groups (plain, BCQ, NCQ) on the same hard 𝑥 each have their own group identifier and are advantage-normalized independently; the plain group on 𝑥 and the reformulated groups on 𝑥 BCQ , 𝑥 NCQ are sampled from different prompt distributions and never share a baseline.

D.2. Baseline implementations 

This subsection describes the implementation of every baseline reported in Tab. 1, Tab. 2, Tab. 3, Tab. 13, and Tab. 4. All baselines share the same student initialization, the same dataset (ZPPO-77K), the same evaluation pipeline, and the same rollout-side hyperparameters (rollout batch size, 

𝐺 S/𝐺 T, sequence-length budget, optimizer, schedule). The gradient-side differs by loss family: RL methods (GRPO, GRPO †, ZPPO, Hint, Prefix) split the per-step rollout batch into 𝐼 equal-sized mini-batches and apply 𝐼 gradient updates on the clipped surrogate (with PPO inner-epoch = 1 

so no rollout token is re-iterated), while distillation methods (Off-Distill, On-Distill) apply a single gradient update on the entire per-step rollout batch under the JSD imitation loss. Each rollout token therefore enters the gradient exactly once per rollout step under both families. 

Off-policy distillation. We instantiate the standard teacher-trajectory imitation paradigm of Hinton-style knowledge distillation [ 17 ] as adapted to sequence models by Sanh et al. [19] , with two modifications motivated by the RL post-training setting: (O1) we filter the teacher’s trajectories by correctness using the same rule-based reward as ZPPO before they enter the imitation loss, instead of imitating the teacher unconditionally; (O2) we draw teacher trajectories online on every rollout step , with no precomputed pool, so that the buffer ablation in Tab. 3 is not confounded by a one-time amortized teacher pre-compute. F.5. Hint and Prefix experimental setup and results 

This subsection details the implementation of the Hint and Prefix baselines reported in Tab. 4 and discusses the numerical pattern that the inline table summarizes. Hint follows the Guide-GRPO [ 93 ]/ SEELE [ 52 ] family: the teacher generates an answer-free hint from a correct teacher trace, the hint is appended to the prompt inside a tag, and the student samples a fully on-policy rollout from the hint-augmented prompt. Prefix follows BREAD [ 51 ] (with StepHint [ 50 ] as another member of the same family): a correct teacher rollout is truncated to a token-fraction prefix and forced as a shared anchor for a branched group of 𝐺 S student continuations, with group-relative advantages computed over the branched group. For implementation simplicity we omit BREAD’s adaptive Episode Anchor Search (EAS) and fix the prefix fraction at 𝛼 = 0 .4. Per-step algorithm annotations are in Appendix D.2 (Algorithms 5 and 6). 

Setup. Both baselines share the prompt replay buffer (admission, graduation, and capacity identical to ZPPO), the same student initialization, the same training dataset, and the same shared training pipeline as ZPPO. Per-method compute is bounded by the GRPO †–ZPPO range reported in Tab. 9 (within the same training-budget convention), rather than listed as separate rows. Each baseline differs from ZPPO only in how it uses a teacher-correct response on hard questions: 

• Hint: a single correct teacher rollout 𝑦 (+) T is converted by the teacher into an answer-free hint and appended to the prompt inside an explicit <hint> · · · </hint> tag. The student then samples its rollout from this hint-augmented prompt; the rollout itself is fully student-generated and the gradient is on-policy at the response-token level (the prompt is augmented with teacher-derived hint text). (Sec. D.2, “Hint baseline” for the exact prompt template.) 

• Prefix: a single correct teacher rollout 𝑦 (+) T is truncated to its first 𝛼 = 0 .4 token-fraction and forced as a starting prefix to the student’s rollout; the student then samples its continuation autoregressively. The teacher-forced prefix tokens are masked from the policy-gradient summation following BREAD (E5 in Appendix D.2), so only the student-continuation tokens enter the gradient as targets. The remaining distinction from ZPPO is the content of the training signal: Prefix supplies only a positive continuation cue (continue a correct teacher trace), without a paired wrong candidate to discriminate against (as BCQ provides) or the student’s own collective wrong rollouts to avoid (as NCQ provides). Neither baseline runs BCQ or NCQ. All other hyperparameters (Tab. 8) are identical to ZPPO. 

Result pattern. Tab. 4 surfaces three things at 2B that the appendix lets us interpret precisely. 

> 57 Zone of Proximal Policy Optimization

(i) Hint helps a moderate amount on VLM but plateaus. GRPO †+Hint gains +1 .2 pp on VLM over GRPO †, but is still 0.4 pp below GRPO †+BCQ and 1.6 pp below ZPPO. The student learns from teacher-side guidance, but the signal is one-sided: it never has to compare a correct teacher candidate against its own wrong candidate, and it never sees its own wrong rollouts collected as negatives. The gain therefore saturates below BCQ and the full BCQ+NCQ recipe. 

(ii) Hint barely transfers to LLM or Video. GRPO †+Hint comes in at 47 .6 / 61 .6 on LLM / Video, marginal versus GRPO †’s 47 .3 / 61 .9, because the hint acts as a directional shortcut that the student copies without internalizing why the direction is correct, and exercises neither the contrastive discrimination nor the collective-negative avoidance that BCQ and NCQ supply. 

(iii) Prefix is worse on generalization. GRPO †+Prefix loses −1.0 pp on LLM and −0.9 pp on Video relative to GRPO †, while gaining only +0 .3 pp on VLM. With teacher-prefix tokens masked from the policy loss following BREAD (E5), the gradient consumes only student-generated continuation tokens, so the gap is not a gradient-side issue. The issue is what the training signal 

contains : Prefix supplies only a single positive continuation cue per hard question – a correct teacher trace the student copies and extends as a continuation shortcut – without any paired wrong candidate to discriminate against (as BCQ provides) or any aggregation of the student’s own collective wrong rollouts to avoid (as NCQ provides). The gain on LLM and Video benchmarks therefore lags BCQ/NCQ, since those held-out suites stress discrimination and self-correction behaviors rather than continuation from a known-good anchor. It all comes down to the gap between the best prompt-injection baseline (Hint, 47 .6 / 60 .4 / 61 .6)and full ZPPO ( 50 .4 / 62 .0 / 63 .2): +2 .8 / + 1 .6 / + 1 .6 pp. We read that gap as the empirical price of any prompt-side method that drops (i) the discriminative two-candidate construction of BCQ, where one teacher-correct trace and one student-wrong trace are anonymized and shuffled with no correctness label, and (ii) the collective-negative aggregation of NCQ, where every wrong student rollout on the same question is surfaced together. Hint gives only an answer-free guidance signal with no negative side, and Prefix forces a continuation from a single teacher trace and surfaces no negatives. Neither comes close to BCQ or NCQ on either axis. 

F.6. Benchmark-selection robustness via cluster bootstrap 

We complement the training runs with a benchmark-level cluster bootstrap covering every pairwise comparison reported in the main paper (Tabs. 1, 10, 2, 11, 3, 12, 13), not just the headline ZPPO − GRPO † delta. The procedure quantifies how robust each macro-level delta is to which exact benchmarks are included in the family. For each (scale , benchmark family ) block we resample the benchmarks with replacement 10 ,000 times and re-compute the macro-average delta on each resample, then report the macro-average Δ, its 95% percentile CI, and the win-rate (number of benchmarks where ZPPO > baseline). The reported Δ is this benchmark-level macro-average (the mean of the per-benchmark deltas computed from unrounded scores), so it can differ by ≤0.1 pp from the Avg-row Δ printed in the main tables, which is the difference of the rounded column averages. Within each block we draw a single shared benchmark resample per iteration that is reused across all columns and fix the RNG seed, so two columns that encode the identical comparison receive identical intervals and the cross-column deltas stay coherent. The Best non-ZPPO baseline is the strongest non-ZPPO row selected on the full sample and then bootstrapped under the same shared resample (it may be the untrained Base, e.g. on 4B/ 9B Video). Tab. 14 reports CIs for the main-result comparisons; Tab. 15 reports CIs for the ablation comparisons.

NCQ audit. The NCQ prompt is constructed differently from the BCQ prompt: only the rewritten 

wrong student rollouts ( student_neg_compressed ) appear in the candidate block, together with the listed parsed final answers; the rewritten teacher rollout ( teacher_pos_compressed ) is logged for diagnostic purposes but never inserted into the NCQ prompt. We verified this directly on every NCQ prompt in the snapshots: 8/8 student_neg_compressed traces appear verbatim in the corresponding prompt, 0/4 teacher_pos_compressed traces appear in any prompt. The audit table for NCQ (Tab. 17) therefore changes meaning: match-neg is the rate at which the student repeats one of its own listed wrong answers (the NCQ failure mode the prompt asks it to avoid), and match-pos is the rate at which the student’s boxed string matches the rewritten teacher’s boxed string even though the teacher trace was not shown to it (the student arriving at the correct answer independently). The reading of the table is now: 

• Under a trivial avoidance pattern (e.g. the rewriter compressing the listed negatives so coarsely that the student can avoid them by emitting any non-listed string), NCQ accuracy should be high but match-pos should be near zero. 

• Under a rewriter-correctness pattern (the rewriter turning student_neg_compressed into a teacher-style correct trace), the prompt would now contain the answer, match-neg should drop sharply across all scales, and NCQ accuracy should be high at every scale. The numbers are not consistent with either pattern. NCQ accuracy is intermediate and scale-dependent (1.2% at 0.8B, 22% /35% /31% at 2/4/9B). match-neg at 0.8B is 82 .7% – i.e. when the smallest student is given its own collected wrong rollouts and told to avoid them, it repeats one of them as its final answer most of the time. Only as the student scales up does match-neg collapse (2.2% at 2B, 0.7% /0.2% at 4/9B), in lockstep with NCQ accuracy growing. This shape is what one would expect from a student that gradually learns to discriminate against its own previously listed wrong answers; it is harder to reconcile with a static avoidance pattern, where one would expect match-neg to be uniformly small from the first scale. 

Caveats on the audit. Three caveats apply to both tables. First, the comparison uses exact normalized ∖boxed-string match, so a student rollout that arrives at the correct answer through different surface text (e.g. “ C” versus the prose description spelled out by the rewritten teacher) is counted as other rather than as a match. Second, the snapshots cover the convergence regime rather than the entire training trajectory; convergence-regime numbers do not by themselves rule out a different match pattern earlier in training, although they are the regime in which any persistent match behaviour would have had the most time to be exploited. Third, on MCQ-style benchmarks with small answer spaces, NCQ’s collective negative listing can in principle help by elimination as well as by discrimination; the audit table cannot disentangle these two contributions, and the open-ended worked examples in Figs. 26, 27, 29, and 30 (free-form counting and chart-reading, all with open-ended numeric answer spaces) are where elimination is least likely to substitute for discrimination.

G.3. Discussion 

Two observations on the 27 B teacher link Tab. 18 and Tab. 19–Tab. 21 back to the per-benchmark structure of ZPPO’s gains. 

(1) The 27 B teacher’s accuracy varies sharply across benchmarks. Even at 27 B, the teacher is far from saturated on the hard math/reasoning slices: HLE ( 16 .0), OCR ZH (33 .5), BabyV ( 30 .4), and OCR EN (55 .7) all sit well below the macro-averages. BCQ candidates can therefore exist on hard benchmarks (HLE, BabyV, . . . ) because the teacher still occasionally solves what the student cannot, but the candidate pool is intrinsically thinner there than on saturated benchmarks (AI2D, MMLU-Rd, . . . ), and ZPPO’s gains correspondingly correlate with how often the teacher–student gap on a benchmark is non-trivial but non-empty. 

(2) A ZPPO-trained 9B student approaches the 27 B teacher on several benchmarks. 

Per-benchmark, the 9B+ZPPO row of Tab. 19–Tab. 21 sits within ≤1.0 pp of the 27 B teacher on AIME25 ( 70 .0 vs. 70 .0), OCR EN (56 .7 vs. 55 .7), InfoVQA, MultiCh, MVerse, MVista, MM-Vet, and WeMath. We do not interpret these proximate values as the student strictly surpassing the teacher: the per-run noise band on single ZPPO runs is comparable to the residual gaps. The largest remaining gaps (HLE, VBlind, VMME) line up with benchmarks where the 27 B teacher itself is least saturated – exactly the benchmarks on which BCQ has the least leverage because the teacher cannot supply many extra solved instances the student is missing. Putting the two together, the BCQ-pool argument can be read straight off Tab. 18–Tab. 21. Under the 27 B teacher we use throughout, ZPPO’s per-benchmark gains follow wherever the teacher–student capability gap is non-trivial but non-empty, and by 9B the student has closed in on the teacher across a good part of the suite.
